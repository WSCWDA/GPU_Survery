\section{Introduction}

Modern GPU-accelerated systems increasingly exhibit an ``I/O wall'': model training, graph analytics, and interactive data processing often stall on data movement rather than arithmetic throughput. Early heterogeneous stacks delegated almost all storage interaction to host CPUs, with GPUs acting as passive consumers of memory-resident batches. This organization was reasonable when PCIe bandwidth and kernel launch overhead dominated, but it becomes suboptimal under contemporary NVMe arrays, RDMA-capable fabrics, and multi-GPU nodes \cite{nvidia_gds_blog,pcie_spec,nvme_spec}.

A first major inflection was GPU-aware software that exposed file-like semantics in device code, exemplified by GPUfs \cite{silberstein2013gpufs}. A second inflection is direct-path production I/O, in which GPUDirect Storage (GDS) bypasses host bounce buffers and reduces CPU mediation \cite{nvidia_gds_overview,nvidia_gds_best_practices}. A third inflection is GPU-initiated and GPU-scheduled access to remote or disaggregated storage, as explored by BaM and recent file-system research \cite{bam2024,geminifs2025}.

Despite rapid progress, existing discussions are fragmented across architecture guides, conference papers, and vendor documentation. This survey makes three contributions. First, it proposes a structured taxonomy of GPU-integrated I/O architectures centered on control-plane ownership and data-plane realization. Second, it distills system-level insights that explain when ``direct I/O'' improves end-to-end performance and when it merely shifts bottlenecks. Third, it outlines research opportunities for robust, portable, and multi-tenant GPU-driven storage.
